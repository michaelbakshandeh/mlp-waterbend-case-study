# Macro Index Methodology and Source Selection for Fast Casual Restaurant Analysis

## Purpose

This analysis uses public macro data to construct demand, price, input-cost, labor-cost, and profitability indexes for the fast casual restaurant ecosystem. The objective is not to replace company filings, but to create externally observable indicators that can be compared across brands and used to estimate reported line items before, or in parallel with, company disclosure. The model is particularly focused on Shake Shack, CAVA, and Dutch Bros, with company actuals from `actuals.xlsx` used to calibrate macro signals into company-level estimates.

The core idea is that fast casual performance is shaped by a small number of economic forces: consumer demand, menu pricing, food input inflation, labor cost pressure, and operating leverage. Public macro data does not observe each company perfectly, but carefully selected indexes can isolate these forces more cleanly than broader restaurant or consumer indicators. The model therefore favors macro series that are close to the relevant operating exposure: limited-service restaurant sales for the revenue pool, limited-service meals CPI for menu pricing, brand-relevant PPIs for food inputs, QCEW limited-service payroll and wages for labor, and geography-weighted store footprints for regional exposure.

## Data Sources and Blind Spots

The model uses BLS CPI data for menu-price pressure, especially `CUUR0000SEFV02`, the CPI-U index for limited-service meals and snacks. This series is closer to the pricing environment faced by fast casual and quick-service brands than headline CPI or broad food-away-from-home CPI. Its blind spot is that it still represents a category average. It does not know a specific brand's price architecture, discounting, loyalty offers, delivery mix, item mix, or local market pricing actions.

FRED/BLS data is used for limited-service restaurant sales and food/input cost indexes. The revenue universe is represented by `SM72251XUSN`, retail sales for limited-service eating places. Food input pressure is proxied using PPIs such as food manufacturing, coffee, beef and veal, processed foods and feeds, and fresh vegetables. The advantage is that these indexes are official, recurring, and economically interpretable. The limitation is that PPI baskets are not the same as company purchasing ledgers. They may miss contracted pricing, hedging, distribution costs, vendor rebates, packaging, freight, and mix shifts inside the menu.

BLS QCEW data is used for limited-service restaurant employment, payroll, and average weekly wage data. The script pulls NAICS 722513, limited-service restaurants, and expands quarterly wage and employment records into monthly observations. For national profitability work, QCEW wage bill data is treated as observed labor dollars. For ticker-level modeling, state-level average weekly wages are weighted by each brand's store footprint. The major blind spot is reporting lag: QCEW is rich but delayed. It also captures wage pressure and payroll levels, not store-level staffing efficiency, turnover, scheduling, training burden, or labor productivity.

Google Trends is used as a brand-level demand and awareness proxy. The script pulls search interest for brand names or entity identifiers, maps those columns to tickers, and aggregates weekly data into company fiscal quarters. This is valuable because it is high-frequency and brand-specific. Its blind spots are important: Google normalizes search interest to the highest-interest period in the selected window, so values are relative rather than absolute. Search spikes can reflect negative news, store openings, media events, or investor attention rather than restaurant traffic.

Store-count data is used to weight regional and state macro signals toward each brand's actual footprint. Dutch Bros, Shake Shack, and CAVA do not have the same geographic exposure, so a national macro average can misstate the cost or wage environment that each brand actually faces. The blind spot is that store-count data may be approximate, stale, or manually estimated for some states. It also treats stores as equal units, even though volumes, formats, urban/suburban mix, and maturity differ.

Finally, `actuals.xlsx` provides company fiscal-quarter actuals for restaurant sales, labor, food and distribution, other COGS, and restaurant-level profit. These actuals anchor the macro indexes to company-specific dollar levels through capture ratios. The blind spot is that calibration depends on capture-ratio stability. Rapid unit growth, pricing resets, menu changes, promotional shifts, or margin mix changes can break the historical relationship between a macro proxy and a reported line item.

## Why These Indexes vs. Similar Macro Indexes

|---|---|---|---|

Broad food-away-from-home blends full-service restaurants, where tipping, table service, labor intensity, alcohol mix, and dining occasion differ. Headline CPI is too broad to capture restaurant pricing power. |

Broader wage indexes are timelier in some cases, but they dilute limited-service restaurant labor with unrelated sectors or broader food-service categories. |
| Food input costs | Brand-relevant PPIs, such as coffee for Dutch Bros, beef for Shake Shack, and processed food/meat/vegetable exposure for CAVA | Broad food manufacturing PPI, all-food CPI, grocery CPI | Input inflation is menu-specific. A coffee chain, burger concept, and Mediterranean fast casual brand face different commodity exposures, so brand-relevant PPIs are more informative than a single broad food index. |
| Geographic exposure | State or regional macro data weighted by store counts | National CPI, national wage indexes, national averages | Fast casual brands have uneven footprints. A brand concentrated in the West or South may face different wage, rent, and pricing conditions than a national average suggests. Store-weighting aligns the macro signal with the operating base. |
| Demand and awareness | Brand/entity Google Trends search interest | Consumer sentiment, restaurant confidence, broad restaurant demand indexes | Search interest is brand-specific and higher frequency. Broad sentiment or restaurant demand indexes can identify consumer backdrop, but they miss share shifts and brand-level traffic momentum. |

The common theme is exposure matching. The selected indexes are not chosen because they are perfect; they are chosen because they sit closer to the economic driver being estimated. A narrower and slightly noisier series can be more useful than a broader and cleaner one if it better matches the company's revenue model, menu architecture, labor base, or geography.

## Methodology for Constructing the Macro Indexes

The model first standardizes index-style data by rebasing CPI and PPI series to 100 at the first available observation in the analysis window. This makes different indexes comparable as growth measures even when the raw index levels use different base years or scales. Non-index level data, such as limited-service restaurant sales, is preserved in dollar terms and separately converted into a revenue index when needed.

Monthly and weekly macro panels are then aligned to each company's fiscal-quarter calendar. The `aggregate_to_quarters` function assigns observations to the relevant fiscal quarter and aggregates them using either a mean or sum, depending on the economic meaning of the series. Price, wage, and search-interest series are generally averaged across the quarter. Revenue-like series are summed when they represent activity levels.

For partially observed quarters, the model uses a quarter-to-date projection. It compares the elapsed portion of the current quarter with the comparable elapsed portion from the prior-year quarter, estimates the QTD growth rate, and applies that growth rate to the remaining prior-year observations. This allows the model to form a preliminary quarterly estimate without waiting for a full quarter of data.

The `build_estimates` function creates walk-forward estimates. For each ticker, it uses comparable historical quarters and trailing growth that would have been knowable before the target period. This is important because the estimate is intended to behave like a point-in-time forecasting tool rather than a hindsight fit. The growth estimate is carried forward when necessary, and the model distinguishes realized, partial, and forecast periods.

Revenue estimation uses two complementary approaches. One approach combines Google Trends demand with limited-service menu pricing, multiplying the demand proxy by the price proxy to form a revenue-like index. Another approach uses the limited-service restaurant sales macro series directly. Both are then calibrated to company actuals using capture ratios. A capture ratio measures the relationship between the macro/index proxy and the reported company line item. The `scale_by_capture` function projects this capture relationship forward and scales the index estimate into company-level dollars.

Cost estimates follow the same principle. Food and distribution costs are modeled with brand-relevant PPIs, aggregated to fiscal quarters and scaled to reported food and distribution actuals. Labor is modeled using QCEW state-level limited-service wages, weighted by each brand's store footprint, then aggregated to quarters and scaled to reported labor actuals. Other COGS or opex is estimated from historical company actuals using comparable-quarter growth when a direct macro index is less reliable.

The restaurant-level profit estimate is constructed as estimated revenue minus estimated labor, estimated food/distribution, and estimated other opex. This keeps the profit bridge interpretable: changes can be traced back to pricing, demand, food inflation, wage pressure, or operating-cost assumptions.

## Ticker-Level Estimation Methodology and Run-Rate Treatment

The ticker-level model starts with the company fiscal calendar in `actuals.xlsx`. Each macro panel is rolled into the exact fiscal-quarter windows for each ticker, rather than forcing all companies into calendar quarters. This matters because the reported quarters for Shake Shack, CAVA, and Dutch Bros do not necessarily line up perfectly with month-end macro releases. The model therefore treats the fiscal calendar as the unit of reporting and maps weekly or monthly macro observations into each company's `Period Start` and `Period End`.

For each ticker and quarter, the model classifies the period as realized, partial, or forecast. Realized periods have macro data through the end of the quarter. Partial periods have begun but do not yet have a full quarter of macro observations. Forecast periods have not yet begun or have no direct observations. This classification is important because the model should not accidentally use future information when constructing an estimate.

The run-rate logic is applied to partially observed quarters. When only part of a quarter is available, the model compares the elapsed observations in the current quarter with the same elapsed portion of the comparable prior-year quarter. It then calculates quarter-to-date growth and applies that growth rate to the remaining prior-year observations to complete the quarter. In practical terms, if six weeks of search data are available in a thirteen-week quarter, the model compares those six weeks with the first six weeks of the prior-year comparable quarter, estimates the QTD growth rate, and uses that rate to run-rate the remaining seven weeks. This creates a complete quarterly macro estimate without waiting for the full reporting period.

For fully forecasted quarters, the model uses a walk-forward comparable-period method. The `build_estimates` function looks back one year, uses the comparable quarter as the base, and applies trailing growth that would have been knowable before the target quarter. The output keeps three separate concepts: `actual`, which is the observed or aggregated macro value; `estimate`, which is the model-implied value for periods requiring projection; and `growth`, which is the historical growth rate used to create the estimate. This separation makes it easier to audit whether a number comes from observed data or from the estimation layer.

The revenue estimate is built in two ways. The first is a demand-and-price index: Google Trends captures brand-level search interest, while limited-service meals CPI captures menu-price inflation. Multiplying the demand proxy by the price proxy creates a revenue-like macro index. The second approach uses the limited-service restaurant sales series directly as a broader category revenue pool. In both cases, the model merges the macro index with reported company sales and calculates a capture ratio, defined as macro/index value divided by reported restaurant sales. The projected capture ratio is then used to translate future macro estimates into company-level revenue dollars.

Food and distribution estimates use the same capture-scaling framework, but the macro signal changes by ticker. Dutch Bros is mapped to coffee PPI, Shake Shack to beef and veal PPI, and CAVA to processed foods/meats and related food input PPIs. Each selected input-cost series is aggregated into the company's fiscal quarter, estimated where needed, merged with reported food and distribution actuals, and scaled forward through projected capture. This preserves company-specific calibration while letting the forward estimate respond to public food-cost inflation.

Labor estimates use QCEW state-level average weekly wages for limited-service restaurants. The model weights those state wage series by each brand's store footprint, producing a ticker-specific labor pressure index. That blended wage index is aggregated to fiscal quarters and merged with reported labor actuals. As with revenue and food costs, the historical capture relationship converts the wage index into estimated labor dollars. This approach captures wage-rate pressure in the markets where each brand operates, while the company actuals calibration helps account for differences in staffing model and labor intensity.

Other opex is treated differently because there is no single clean government macro index that maps well to the reported "Other Costs in COGS" line. Instead, the model uses company actuals and comparable-quarter growth. It looks back over a four-quarter window, estimates trailing growth, and applies that growth to the comparable prior-year value. This is a simpler run-rate approach, but it avoids forcing a weak macro proxy into a line item that may include occupancy, utilities, supplies, repairs, delivery fees, and other operating costs.

The final ticker-level profit estimate is a bridge, not a standalone black box. For each ticker and fiscal quarter, estimated restaurant-level profit equals estimated revenue minus estimated labor, estimated food and distribution, and estimated other opex. This structure allows the estimate to be reviewed driver by driver. If a profit estimate changes, the analyst can identify whether the change came from brand demand, category sales, menu pricing, food inflation, wage inflation, or the other-opex run rate.

## Reporting Lag and Granularity by Source

The model uses indexes with different publication speeds and observation frequencies, so the estimate quality varies by line item. Faster series, such as Google Trends and monthly CPI/PPI data, can support more current run-rate estimates. Slower series, especially QCEW, are richer and more industry-specific but require a longer projection window.

The practical hierarchy is therefore: Google Trends, CPI, PPI, and Census retail sales provide the most current read on demand, pricing, input costs, and category revenue; QCEW provides the most detailed labor data but with a longer lag; company actuals and store counts provide calibration rather than independent high-frequency signals. This is why the model uses run-rate methods for partial quarters and capture scaling for ticker-level dollar estimates.

## Why These Indexes Are Useful for Fast Casual Analysis

These indexes are useful because they separate the fast casual ecosystem into observable operating drivers. Company revenue growth can be decomposed into pricing, demand or awareness, and category sales momentum. Margin pressure can be decomposed into food input inflation, wage pressure, and other operating cost trends. That structure is more informative than relying on a single broad macro measure.

They also improve comparability across brands. Shake Shack, CAVA, and Dutch Bros have different menus, store footprints, and growth profiles. A single national restaurant index would flatten those differences. By using brand-relevant PPIs and geography-weighted labor data, the model creates macro signals that better reflect each concept's actual exposure.

The indexes are also defensible because they rely mostly on public, official, recurring data. BLS, QCEW, and FRED series can be audited, refreshed, and compared over time. Google Trends is less official, but it adds a high-frequency brand-specific signal that government data generally lacks. Together, the sources create a practical balance: official macro data for economic fundamentals and search data for more immediate brand demand signals.

The main reason to favor this approach is not that it eliminates uncertainty. It makes uncertainty explicit. Each estimate has a clear data source, a known blind spot, and a traceable conversion from macro signal to company-level line item. For a technical reviewer, that transparency is valuable: the model can be challenged, updated, or refined one driver at a time rather than treated as a black-box forecast.
